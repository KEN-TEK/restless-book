The Journey of St. Augustine to True Life

Beyond Borders — International Students and the Value of Unity

To be an international student is to live between worlds — carrying the culture of one’s homeland while adapting to the rhythms of a foreign land. It means balancing hope with uncertainty and learning to walk the delicate line between belonging and isolation. International students are not merely learners of academic subjects but apprentices of life, discovering resilience, patience, and courage far from home.

Just as St. Augustine, in Confessions, recalls receiving support to pursue his studies in Carthage, Tunisia, so too are many international students today granted scholarships that carry them beyond the borders of their birthplaces. They leave behind families, friends, and cherished traditions, stepping into an unfamiliar future filled with both promise and challenge.

At first glance, one might believe that success rests solely on personal effort — moving swiftly through studies, adapting rapidly to new cultures, and chasing ambitions with determination. Yet, as Augustine’s own journey reveals, brilliance alone cannot sustain someone for long. To go far requires the strength of community, the wisdom of mentors, and the companionship of trusted friends.

In the same way, international students thrive when they cultivate networks of support — from fellow scholars to hospitable host communities. Their success is measured not only by academic triumphs but also by the depth of relationships that sustain them. This truth echoes the enduring wisdom of the African proverb:

“If you want to go fast, go alone. If you want to go far, go together.”

The Power of Belonging

In August 2024, a young Ugandan student applied for a government scholarship. Like many of his peers, he dreamed of pursuing knowledge abroad — of building a future that would honour both his family and his country. His destination was Algeria, a land he had never known but one that now carried the promise of education and growth.

However, this story began long before Algeria. Even in high school, he preferred to work alone. Independence, to him, was survival. He believed walking alone was faster. But Algeria would reveal a different truth.

Months after arrival, he remained isolated — fulfilling academic requirements but never entering student life. His solitude, once a source of strength, began eroding his spirit. As St. Augustine once wrote: “There is no greater misfortune than to be without friends.”

By June 2025, loneliness had grown unbearable. He disappeared from his room, leaving behind his belongings. For three days, fear gripped the campus. Yet, in that darkness, a light emerged — the power of belonging.

Students and authorities across Algeria united in the search. From Jijel to Bejaïa, from embassies to international student networks — hearts joined in solidarity. When he was found alive, weak but safe, there was no anger, only tears of joy. What had begun as one man’s despair became a community’s victory.

“Two are better than one, for if they fall, one will lift up the other.” — St. Augustine

His recovery revealed a profound truth: while solitude promises speed, it rarely grants endurance. Belonging sustains, restores, and carries us further than we could ever go alone.

From Crisis to Vision

The disappearance of that student in June 2025 shook the international student community in Algeria to its core. Yet from this crisis arose a vision. Mr. John Bosco Tumusiime refused to let the memory fade. He realized that students abroad need more than certificates — they need belonging. Thus, he founded the Know Your Neighbour Initiative.

As Friedrich Nietzsche once wrote: “He who has a why to live can bear almost any how.” Bosco discovered his why — to ensure that no student ever feels abandoned in loneliness again.

The Genesis of Know Your Neighbour Initiative

It began on 16 November 2024, during Marhaba Day in Algeria. Bosco noticed that Uganda was missing from the official programme. With humility and courage, he requested to speak — and when he did, his words painted a vivid picture of Uganda: the Pearl of Africa. His heartfelt message touched many and quietly planted a seed that would later grow into the initiative.

Working with Peace Campus Ambassadors and colleagues such as Rogers Kawenja, Mukisa Emmanuel, and Zziwa Charles, Bosco began turning compassion into action — helping those in need, fostering friendship, and showing that generosity transcends borders.

In time, students from across Algeria — Blida, Constantine, Oran, Tlemcen, Annaba, and more — joined hands. Uganda’s name shone brightly, not just for its beauty but for its spirit of cooperation. When crisis struck again, this same unity became a shield of hope and proof that together, we are stronger.

The Goals of the Initiative

The Know Your Neighbour Initiative is guided by five core objectives:

  • 1. Promote Uganda’s Tourism and Culture: showcasing Uganda’s beauty and diversity through cultural nights, exhibitions, and festivals — embodying St. Augustine’s insight: “The world is a book, and those who do not travel read only one page.”
  • 2. Strengthen Youth Engagement through Sports and Recreation: through friendly matches and tournaments that build teamwork and friendship. “We learn better in friendship than in fear.”
  • 3. Build Meaningful Friendships through Dialogue and Knowledge Sharing: fostering understanding through conversation, storytelling, and exchange. “When we speak and listen with love, we build the city of God even among men.”
  • 4. Carry Out Acts of Compassion and Solidarity: ensuring that no student walks alone. “Love has hands to help others, feet to hasten to the poor and needy.”
  • 5. Promote Trade and Investment: strengthening ties through collaboration and entrepreneurship. “Good works are stronger when done together.”

The Initiative in Motion

From one man’s courage arose a community. From one act of compassion blossomed a movement. Through the support of Mr. Mohamed Benmounemene Salim, Peace Campus Ambassadors, and many students, the initiative became a living proof that true progress is never born of isolation.

Today, it continues to grow — patiently awaiting formal recognition, yet already alive in every act of kindness and every bridge built between Uganda and Algeria. It reminds us all that unity is the highest form of strength.

“If you want to go fast, go alone. If you want to go far, go together.”
